## Prof. Linda's Notes for the Development Team

**On drug class understanding vs. individual drugs:**
The NCLEX increasingly tests drug class mechanisms rather than individual drug facts. A student who understands that beta-blockers work by blocking catecholamine binding at beta-adrenergic receptors can answer questions about any beta-blocker. Build questions that test mechanisms, not just isolated facts.

**On the prototype drug approach:**
For each drug class there is typically one prototype drug that NCLEX uses most frequently. Questions about other class members usually compare them to the prototype. The list above includes prototypes and important exceptions — that is intentional.

**On black box warnings:**
Every drug with a black box warning is high-yield. NCLEX tests whether students know the most serious risk. The openFDA API returns black box warning text — the question generators should specifically use that field.

**On antidotes — extremely high-yield pairs:**
- Opioids: naloxone
- Benzodiazepines: flumazenil
- Warfarin: vitamin K and FFP
- Heparin: protamine sulfate
- Acetaminophen: N-acetylcysteine
- Dabigatran: idarucizumab
- Magnesium toxicity: calcium gluconate
- Digoxin: digoxin immune Fab
- Beta-blocker overdose: glucagon

Build dedicated antidote questions into the pharmacology mode.

**On what I intentionally excluded:**
Highly specialized oncology chemotherapy regimens (CHOP, FOLFOX, etc.) are rarely tested at the RN level. Most topical and ophthalmic antibiotics, OTC antihistamines beyond diphenhydramine and cetirizine, and dermatology-specific agents appear rarely and are lower yield than the drugs listed here.

## 2025–2026 Clinical Updates — Prof. Linda's Addendum

*Added March 2026 after cross-reference against FDA novel drug approvals 2024–2025 and current NCLEX 2026 test plan.*

**On tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound):** Add it. It's everywhere in clinical practice now. Students are seeing patients on it in clinicals and it will appear on NCLEX within the next 1-2 exam cycles. The dual GLP-1/GIP mechanism is worth knowing, but the nursing focus for NCLEX is the same as semaglutide: GI side effects, pancreatitis risk, contraindication in personal/family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN2, and the sleep apnea indication is genuinely new and testable.

**On semaglutide updates:** The CKD indication is clinically significant and aligns with the 2026 test plan's increased emphasis on renal nursing. Update the NCLEX focus note. The oral formulation (Wegovy pill) is also worth noting — students need to know both forms exist.

**On lecanemab:** Lower yield for 2026 NCLEX specifically — exam content lags approvals by 1-2 years typically. But include it as awareness content for the voice assistant and reader. MRI monitoring and ARIA risk are the nursing-specific concerns. Good NGN scenario material.

**On nirsevimab (Beyfortus):** Absolutely add this. It replaced palivizumab as the standard RSV prophylaxis for infants for most institutions in 2023-2024. Students going into pediatric and neonatal nursing need to know the distinction between this (monoclonal antibody, passive immunization, single dose) and traditional vaccines. It's already in NCLEX test bank content from major prep companies.

**On suzetrigine:** Too new for 2026 NCLEX. Approved January 2025 — watch for it in 2027-2028 test plans. Leave off the list for now.

**On the rest of the list:** The core drug classes are stable and correct. NCLEX pharmacology content does not change as quickly as clinical practice — the exam tests principles and established drugs, not cutting-edge approvals. The 319+ drugs already on this list represent comprehensive coverage for the 2026 exam.
